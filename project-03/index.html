Professional Skaters Want You to Buy Their Decks & Shoes

Looking through sponsporship promotion habits for 100 professional skateboarders on social media

By Niall Simonian

Published July 24, 2023

The world of professional skateboarding is unlike all other global sports of its scale. This is only in part due to the fact that professional skateboarders, with no lack of pride and satisfaction, must break local property laws and city ordinances as a matter of routine. Rather, what I am referring to is the very structure of these pro athletes' earnings.

Unlike the stars of the NBA or NFL, skateboarders do not receive annual contracts from any one organization. While they may earn sporadic prize money from competitions, most skateboarders rely on sponsorship deals in order to make a living.

Skateboarding's continued rise in popularity, boosted by the sport's inaugural inclusion in the most recent summer Olympics, has made many of these endorsement deals quite lucrative. This popularity has even attracted a number of high profile, and non-traditional, brand partners. Take Louis Vuitton as an example- in 2022, the company inked a deal on the 4th iteration of a skateboard shoe, retailed at $1300, in partnership with professional skateboarder Lucien Clarke.

That all being said, the majority of brand deals held by professional skateboarders remain very much within the skateboard industry itself. As is the nature of any sponsorship structure, the value of a sponsee depends greatly on their reach and popularity. In the age of social media, there is perhaps no better place to assess that popularity than Instagram. The video friendly features of the platform make it an ideal destination for athletes to post skateboarding clips. Anyone who follows a few pro skaters on Instagram would be quick to confirm that the athletes also frequently share their endorsements in their bios.

Obviously, and as mentioned earlier, most skateboarding sponsorships come from skateboarding companies. In particular, each pro skater usually has an endorsement from a different company for each part of their physical skateboard. For those readers whose only experience with a skateboard is a pre-assembled board from a local Target or Walmart, the following diagram should illustrate the five primary parts of any board.

In addition to receiving free equipment and financial incentives from the companies that produce these parts, skateboarders also appear in skate videos, with the other sponsorees of the company, showcasing their skills on the streets. In this way, a skateboarder is really a member of a number of different teams simultaneously.

There is a common conception that skateboarders have a loyalty, first and foremost, to the company that produces their skateboard deck- as they traditionally collaborate to create a unique design that shares their name and the brand name. So surely, if this is true, skateboarders would choose to promote their deck company more often than any other company. To confirm this, I looked at the profiles of 100 professional skateboarders and which brands they chose to endorse.

Like every other decision in the world of skateboarding, there is always a consideration of “style” that skaters must consider when deciding to include their sponsors or not in their bios. As a result, it turns out that only 55% of the sample had linked sponsors in their bio.

When looking at the 55% of accounts with sponsorship information, my hypothesis that the deck companies would be represented most frequently was true. The next most frequent sponsorship type was the truck companies, and the least frequently represented was the grip tape sponsor- grip tape is boring, so this checks out. Additionally, each account had an average of 2.6 non-part related accounts linked; this included clothes companies, personal business ventures, local skateparks, and more.
